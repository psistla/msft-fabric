Here's the consolidation logic before presenting it, so you can validate the grouping:

The 14 components collapse into 6 by natural architectural affinity — **Ingestion + Storage** share the data movement and persistence concern; **Classification + Governance + Catalog** are all data trust controls; **Quality + Observability** are both data health disciplines; **Semantic Layer + Reporting** serve the same consumption surface; **Vector Store + RAG + AI Pipelines + MLOps** are all AI execution concerns; and **Security + Responsible AI + Capacity** are all platform risk controls.

Nothing is eliminated — all 14 original components are preserved inside one of the six.

**Three things this consolidation makes visible that the 14-component view obscures:**

The first is that component 02 (Classification & Governance) is the load-bearing wall for everything else. Every other component either feeds into it or depends on it. If data ownership and classification are not resolved, components 04, 05, and 06 cannot function correctly — not slowly, not partially, but not at all.

The second is that the real-time streaming work inside component 05 is the most likely scope to be over-engineered. It belongs inside the AI pipeline component because the decision of whether to build streaming infrastructure should be driven by the AI latency SLA, not by a general appetite for real-time data. Get that SLA agreed with business stakeholders before the architecture is drawn.

The third is that component 06 is the one most clients underestimate in effort and cost. Responsible AI controls, compliance evidence automation, and capacity governance each require dedicated operational discipline — not just platform configuration. The risk of treating them as checkboxes rather than ongoing practices is the highest single risk in any regulated AI engagement.
